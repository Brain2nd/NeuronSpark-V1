% Abstract
% ============================================================
\begin{abstract}
We present \model{}, a 0.9-billion parameter language model built \emph{entirely} on spiking neural networks (SNNs).
Unlike prior SNN language models that distill from pretrained Transformers, \model{} is trained from scratch using surrogate gradient backpropagation, demonstrating that pure SNN architectures can acquire language modeling capabilities through standard next-token prediction.

Our architecture introduces three key innovations:
(1)~\textbf{Selective State Space SNN Blocks} with input-dependent dynamic parameters $\beta(t), \alpha(t), \vth(t)$, drawing a formal connection between SNN membrane dynamics and selective state space models;
(2)~\textbf{Membrane potential leakage activation}, where PLIFNode neurons output $(1-\beta)\cdot\vpost$ --- the leak current --- as inter-layer signals, naturally weighting fast-responding neurons over slow-memory neurons;
(3)~\textbf{PonderNet adaptive timesteps}, allowing each token to dynamically decide its effective SNN computation depth $K_{\text{eff}} \in [1, K_{\max}]$ via learned geometric-distribution halt probabilities.

\model{}-0.9B is trained on a \emph{small subset} of the available data ($\sim$1.4B tokens of a 10B-token Chinese corpus for pretraining; $\sim$6.5K steps of 3.5M instruction samples for SFT) using custom Triton-fused PLIF kernels on a single NVIDIA DGX Spark.
Even with this limited data budget, the model achieves a pretraining loss of 3.6 and demonstrates basic dialogue capability after supervised fine-tuning, validating the architectural viability of pure SNN language modeling.
To our knowledge, this is the largest SNN language model trained from scratch to date.
We release all code, weights, and training infrastructure under Apache~2.0.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Large language models (LLMs) based on Transformers~\citep{vaswani2017attention} have achieved remarkable success across natural language processing tasks.
However, their quadratic attention mechanism and dense floating-point computation raise fundamental questions about computational efficiency and biological plausibility.
Meanwhile, spiking neural networks (SNNs)~\citep{maass1997networks} --- the ``third generation'' of neural networks --- process information through discrete spikes and temporal dynamics, offering potential advantages in energy efficiency and neuromorphic hardware deployment.

Despite significant progress in SNN-based vision models, SNN language modeling remains largely unexplored.
Existing approaches such as SpikeGPT~\citep{zhu2024spikegpt}, SpikingBERT~\citep{bal2024spikingbert}, and SpikeBERT~\citep{lv2023spikebert} either rely on knowledge distillation from pretrained Transformers or are limited to small-scale models.
A fundamental question remains open: \emph{Can a pure SNN architecture learn language from scratch at scale, using only standard next-token prediction?}

In this work, we answer affirmatively by introducing \model{}, a 0.9B-parameter SNN language model trained from scratch.
Due to compute constraints (single DGX Spark), we train on only $\sim$1.4B tokens of a 10B-token corpus, yet the model acquires basic language generation and dialogue capabilities --- suggesting favorable inductive biases of the architecture.
Our key insight is that the membrane potential dynamics of Leaky Integrate-and-Fire (LIF) neurons can be viewed as a selective state space model~\citep{gu2024mamba}, where the decay rate $\beta$, input gain $\alpha$, and firing threshold $\vth$ serve as input-dependent gating mechanisms analogous to Mamba's selection mechanism.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item We propose the \textbf{Selective State Space SNN Block} with 7 parallel projection paths, computing dynamic $\beta(t), \alpha(t), \vth(t)$ from input signals through learned modulation networks with structured initialization, establishing a formal SNN--SSM duality (Section~\ref{sec:snn-block}).

    \item We introduce \textbf{membrane potential leakage activation} $(1-\beta)\cdot\vpost$ for PLIFNode inter-layer signals, which naturally emphasizes fast-responding neurons and provides implicit temporal-scale weighting (Section~\ref{sec:leakage}).

    \item We design \textbf{PonderNet adaptive timesteps} at each sublayer, enabling per-token dynamic SNN computation depth with geometric-distribution weighting and ponder cost regularization (Section~\ref{sec:pondernet}).

    \item We develop \textbf{Triton-fused PLIF kernels} with per-element and row-parameter variants, performing the entire PLIF forward/backward (including surrogate gradient) in a single kernel launch, plus \textbf{torch.compile-fused modulation and halt weight kernels} (Section~\ref{sec:triton}).

    \item We introduce \textbf{residual centering} and \textbf{lateral inhibition normalization} (divisive normalization) as SNN-native stabilization techniques, along with a two-phase \textbf{natural gradient compensation} for modulation parameters (Sections~\ref{sec:residual-centering}--\ref{sec:natural-gradient}).

    \item We train and release \model{}-0.9B on limited data, demonstrating that a pure SNN can learn language from scratch (Section~\ref{sec:experiments}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Spiking Neural Networks for Language.}
SpikeGPT~\citep{zhu2024spikegpt} proposed a generative SNN language model using binary spike activations, achieving perplexity comparable to small Transformers on limited benchmarks.
However, SpikeGPT retains standard (non-spiking) embedding and output layers.
SpikingBERT~\citep{bal2024spikingbert} and SpikeBERT~\citep{lv2023spikebert} distill BERT representations into spiking architectures, starting from pretrained ANN weights.
All prior approaches operate at $\leq$216M parameters.
\model{} differs by training a 0.9B SNN from random initialization using only next-token prediction, with spiking dynamics throughout (except embedding lookup).

\paragraph{State Space Models.}
Structured State Spaces (S4)~\citep{gu2022efficiently} introduced efficient linear recurrence for sequence modeling.
Mamba~\citep{gu2024mamba} added input-dependent selection, achieving Transformer-competitive performance.
Mamba-2~\citep{dao2024transformers} established a formal duality between SSMs and attention.
We observe that SNN membrane dynamics $V[t] = \beta(t) \cdot V[t-1] + \alpha(t) \cdot I[t]$ are structurally identical to the selective SSM recurrence, with $\beta$ as the decay coefficient and $\alpha$ as the input gate.
The spike-and-reset mechanism adds a discrete nonlinearity absent in continuous SSMs.

\paragraph{Adaptive Computation.}
Adaptive Computation Time (ACT)~\citep{graves2016adaptive} allows networks to vary computation per input.
PonderNet~\citep{banino2021pondernet} improved upon ACT with a geometric distribution prior.
We apply PonderNet at the SNN timestep level within each sublayer: the $K$ frames per token are aggregated with learned halt probabilities, enabling different tokens to use 1 to $K_{\max}$ effective SNN steps.

\paragraph{Surrogate Gradient Training.}
The non-differentiability of spike generation ($\Theta(V - \vth)$) is addressed by surrogate gradient methods~\citep{neftci2019surrogate, zenke2021remarkable}, which replace the Heaviside derivative with smooth approximations.
\model{} uses Sigmoid surrogate gradients ($\alpha=4.0$) throughout, implemented in the SpikingJelly framework~\citep{fang2023spikingjelly}, with custom Triton kernels that fuse the surrogate computation into the sequential scan.

\section{Architecture}
\label{sec:arch}

\subsection{Overview}
\label{sec:overview}

\model{} follows a three-stage pipeline (Figure~\ref{fig:architecture}):
\textbf{(1)~Encode}: Token IDs $\to$ embedding ($D$-dim) $\to$ repeat $K$ times, producing a $(T{\cdot}K, B, D)$ tensor. Gradients flow directly through embedding; the output head reuses the embedding matrix (weight tying~\citep{press2017using}).
\textbf{(2)~SNN Forward}: $L{=}20$ decoder layers with gradient checkpointing, each containing an SNNBlock (attention analogue) and an SNNFFN (MLP analogue), with PonderNet adaptive $K$-frame aggregation. All neuron states reset per sequence.
\textbf{(3)~Decode}: RMSNorm~\citep{zhang2019root} $\to$ output PLIFNode (leakage) $\to$ $K$-frame uniform mean $\to$ projection $\to$ lateral inhibition~\citep{carandini2012normalization} $\to$ tied head $\to$ logits.

Each decoder layer follows a Pre-LN residual pattern matching Qwen3/LLaMA~\citep{touvron2023llama, yang2025qwen3}:
\begin{align}
    \mathbf{h} &\leftarrow \mathbf{h} + \text{center}\big(\text{OutProj}\big(\text{PonderAgg}\big(\text{SNNBlock}\big(\text{PLIFLeak}\big(\text{RMSNorm}(\mathbf{h})\big)\big)\big)\big)\big) \label{eq:layer-block} \\
    \mathbf{h} &\leftarrow \mathbf{h} + \text{center}\big(\text{OutProj}\big(\text{PonderAgg}\big(\text{SNNFFN}\big(\text{PLIFLeak}\big(\text{RMSNorm}(\mathbf{h})\big)\big)\big)\big)\big) \label{eq:layer-ffn}
\end{align}
where $\text{center}(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{x} - \text{mean}(\mathbf{x})$ is residual centering (Section~\ref{sec:residual-centering}).
The residual stream $\mathbf{h} \in \R^{TK \times B \times D}$ carries continuous values throughout; only the SNN sublayers operate on spike/membrane dynamics.

\subsection{PLIF Neuron Dynamics}
\label{sec:plif}

All neurons in \model{} follow the Parametric Leaky Integrate-and-Fire (PLIF) model~\citep{fang2021incorporating}.
We distinguish two variants:

\paragraph{PLIFNode (fixed parameters).}
Used at layer boundaries (input neurons, gate/up neurons, output neuron).
Each has $D$-dimensional (or $D_{\text{ff}}$-dimensional) learnable parameters:
\begin{align}
    \vpre[t] &= \beta \cdot \vpost[t-1] + (1-\beta) \cdot x[t] \label{eq:plif-charge} \\
    s[t] &= \Theta(\vpre[t] - \vth) \label{eq:plif-fire} \\
    \vpost[t] &= \vpre[t] - \vth \cdot s[t] \label{eq:plif-reset}
\end{align}
where $\beta = \sigma(w) \in (0,1)$ with $w \sim \mathcal{N}(\text{logit}(1 - 1/\tau_0),\; 0.5)$ and $\vth \sim \mathcal{U}(0.5 v_0,\; 1.5 v_0)$.
The random initialization creates diversity across dimensions: different neurons have different time constants and firing sensitivities.
Equation~\eqref{eq:plif-reset} implements \emph{soft reset}: the membrane potential is reduced by $\vth$ upon firing, preserving residual charge.

\paragraph{SelectivePLIFNode (dynamic parameters).}
Used inside SNNBlock for $D \cdot N$ hidden neurons.
Parameters $\beta(t), \alpha(t), \vth(t)$ are computed per-step from the input (Section~\ref{sec:snn-block}):
\begin{align}
    V[t] &= \beta(t) \cdot V[t-1] + \alpha(t) \cdot I[t] \label{eq:selective-charge} \\
    s[t] &= \Theta(V[t] - \vth(t)) \label{eq:selective-fire} \\
    V[t] &\mathrel{-}= \vth(t) \cdot s[t] \label{eq:selective-reset}
\end{align}
This is structurally identical to Mamba's selective SSM recurrence $h[t] = \bar{A}(t) \cdot h[t-1] + \bar{B}(t) \cdot x[t]$, with the addition of the spike-and-reset nonlinearity.


\subsection{Membrane Potential Leakage Activation}
\label{sec:leakage}

A critical design choice is the signal transmitted between components.
Standard SNN practice uses binary spikes $s[t] \in \{0,1\}$, but this severely limits gradient flow through the surrogate function's narrow support.
An alternative is the raw membrane potential $\vpost$, but this treats all neurons equally regardless of their temporal dynamics.

We use the \textbf{membrane potential leakage} as inter-layer activation:
\begin{equation}
    \text{leak}[t] = (1 - \beta) \cdot \vpost[t]
    \label{eq:leakage}
\end{equation}
This quantity is the amount of membrane potential that will \emph{dissipate} due to exponential decay before the next input arrives.
Biologically, it corresponds to the leak current through the membrane conductance~\citep{hodgkin1952quantitative, abbott1999lapicque}.

The leakage activation provides natural \textbf{temporal-scale weighting}:
neurons with large $(1-\beta)$ (fast dynamics, short memory) produce proportionally larger signals,
while neurons with small $(1-\beta)$ (slow dynamics, long memory) are implicitly attenuated.
This reweighting is applied at all PLIFNode outputs: input neurons (2 per layer), gate/up neurons in SNNFFN (2 per layer), and the output neuron.

The SelectivePLIFNode \emph{hidden} neurons inside SNNBlock output raw $\vpost$ rather than leakage, because $\beta(t)$ is dynamic (varies per step) and cannot be absorbed into a static downstream weight matrix.
This is a deliberate design choice: leakage scaling applies only at fixed-$\beta$ boundaries.


\subsection{Selective State Space SNN Block}
\label{sec:snn-block}

The SNNBlock is the attention analogue, processing input through $D \cdot N$ hidden spiking neurons with input-dependent parameters.
It computes \textbf{seven parallel projections} from the input leakage signal --- six input projections and one output projection:

\paragraph{Input projections} ($D \to D \cdot N$ or $D \to D$):
\begin{align}
    \mathbf{I}[t] &= W_{\text{in}} \cdot \text{leak}[t] & &\in \R^{DN} & &\text{(input current)} \label{eq:block-I} \\
    \beta(t) &= \sigma\big(W_\beta \cdot \text{leak}[t] + \mathbf{b}_\beta\big) & &\in (0,1)^{DN} & &\text{(decay rate)} \label{eq:block-beta} \\
    \alpha(t) &= \text{softplus}\big(W_\alpha \cdot \text{leak}[t] + \mathbf{b}_\alpha\big) & &\in \R_+^{DN} & &\text{(input gain)} \label{eq:block-alpha} \\
    \vth(t) &= \vmin + \big|W_{\text{th}} \cdot \text{leak}[t] + \mathbf{b}_{\text{th}}\big| & &\in [\vmin, \infty)^{DN} & &\text{(firing threshold)} \label{eq:block-vth} \\
    \mathbf{g}[t] &= \sigma\big(W_{\text{gate}} \cdot \text{leak}[t]\big) & &\in (0,1)^D & &\text{(output gate)} \label{eq:block-gate} \\
    \mathbf{I}_{\text{skip}}[t] &= W_{\text{skip}} \cdot \text{leak}[t] & &\in \R^D & &\text{(skip connection)} \label{eq:block-skip}
\end{align}
The modulation projections $W_\beta, W_\alpha, W_{\text{th}}$ are initialized at $0.1\times$ the scale of $W_{\text{in}}$, ensuring that $\beta(t), \alpha(t), \vth(t)$ are dominated by their respective biases at the start of training, providing a stable initialization.

\paragraph{Hidden neuron dynamics.}
The $D \cdot N$ hidden neurons follow SelectivePLIF (Eqs.~\ref{eq:selective-charge}--\ref{eq:selective-reset}), computed via fused Triton PLIF kernels (Section~\ref{sec:triton}).

\paragraph{Output projection} ($D \cdot N \to D$):
\begin{equation}
    \text{out}[t] = W_{\text{out}} \cdot \vpost[t] \odot \mathbf{g}[t] + \mathbf{I}_{\text{skip}}[t]
    \label{eq:block-out}
\end{equation}
Note: the output uses $\vpost$ (not leakage) from the hidden neurons, because $\beta(t)$ is dynamic.
The gate $\mathbf{g}$ provides multiplicative control over which dimensions pass through.
The skip connection $\mathbf{I}_{\text{skip}}$ ensures gradient flow even when all hidden neurons are silent.

\paragraph{Structured initialization.}
The modulation biases $\mathbf{b}_\beta, \mathbf{b}_\alpha, \mathbf{b}_{\text{th}}$ receive carefully designed initialization (details in Appendix~\ref{app:init}):
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\mathbf{b}_\beta$: logit-spaced across $N$ groups targeting $\beta \in [0.80, 0.99]$ (multi-timescale), with per-dimension $\mathcal{N}(0, 0.1)$ perturbation to break symmetry.
    \item $\mathbf{b}_\alpha$: initialized near $\text{softplus}^{-1}(1.0) = 0.5413$ so initial $\alpha \approx 1$.
    \item $\mathbf{b}_{\text{th}}$: calibrated from the stationary variance $\sigma_V = \sqrt{p/3} \cdot \sqrt{1 - \beta^{2K}}$ with target firing rates linearly spaced from 25\% to 8\% across $N$ groups.
    \item $W_{\text{in}}$: rows scaled by $\sqrt{1 - \beta^2}$ per $N$-group, compensating for the larger steady-state variance of slow neurons.
    \item $W_{\text{out}}$: columns scaled by $1/\sqrt{p_{\text{fire}}}$ per $N$-group, equalizing the contribution of neurons with different firing rates.
\end{itemize}


\subsection{SNN Feed-Forward Network}
\label{sec:snn-ffn}

The SNNFFN mirrors the SwiGLU MLP~\citep{touvron2023llama} with spiking neurons replacing the activation function:
\begin{align}
    \text{gate\_leak} &= (1-\beta_g) \cdot \vpost\big(\text{PLIF}_{\text{gate}}(W_{\text{gate}} \cdot \text{leak})\big) \label{eq:ffn-gate} \\
    \text{up\_leak} &= (1-\beta_u) \cdot \vpost\big(\text{PLIF}_{\text{up}}(W_{\text{up}} \cdot \text{leak})\big) \label{eq:ffn-up} \\
    \text{out} &= W_{\text{down}} \cdot (\text{gate\_leak} \odot \text{up\_leak}) + W_{\text{skip}} \cdot \text{leak} \label{eq:ffn-out}
\end{align}
where $W_{\text{gate}}, W_{\text{up}} \in \R^{D_{\text{ff}} \times D}$, $W_{\text{down}} \in \R^{D \times D_{\text{ff}}}$, and $W_{\text{skip}} \in \R^{D \times D}$.

The element-wise product of two leakage signals (Eq.~\ref{eq:ffn-out}) replaces $\text{SiLU}(x) \odot x$ gating in SwiGLU~\citep{shazeer2020glu}.
Both PLIF neurons provide implicit nonlinearity through the integrate-fire-reset cycle; their leakage outputs carry temporal dynamics that pure activation functions cannot express.

$W_{\text{down}}$ is initialized with $1/\sqrt{L}$ scaling to prevent gradient explosion through deep residual chains.
Implementation optimizations (merged matmul, merged PLIF scan) are detailed in Appendix~\ref{app:engineering}.


\subsection{PonderNet Adaptive Timesteps}
\label{sec:pondernet}

Each token is represented as $K$ SNN frames.
Rather than uniformly averaging all $K$ frames, we learn per-frame halt probabilities following PonderNet~\citep{banino2021pondernet}:
\begin{align}
    p_k &= \sigma\big(W_{\text{halt}} \cdot \text{frame}_k + b_{\text{halt}}\big) \in (0, 1) \label{eq:ponder-halt} \\
    S_k &= \textstyle\prod_{j=1}^{k-1} (1 - p_j) \quad \text{(survival probability)} \label{eq:ponder-survive} \\
    \lambda_k &= p_k \cdot S_k \quad \text{(geometric distribution weight)} \label{eq:ponder-lambda} \\
    \hat{\lambda}_k &= \lambda_k \big/ \textstyle\sum_{k'} \lambda_{k'} \quad \text{(normalized)} \label{eq:ponder-norm} \\
    \text{output} &= \textstyle\sum_k \hat{\lambda}_k \cdot \text{frame}_k \label{eq:ponder-agg}
\end{align}

The \textbf{expected timesteps} $\Expect[K] = \sum_k k \cdot \hat{\lambda}_k$ serves as a ponder cost regularizer added to the training loss with weight $\lambda_{\text{ponder}} = 0.01$, encouraging the model to use fewer steps for ``easy'' tokens while allocating more computation to complex ones.
PonderNet is applied independently at each sublayer (SNNBlock and SNNFFN), giving $2L = 40$ adaptive aggregation points.

\paragraph{Initialization.}
$W_{\text{halt}}$ is initialized with Xavier uniform $\times 0.01$ (small weights) and $b_{\text{halt}} = -3.5$, giving $\sigma(-3.5) \approx 0.03$.
At initialization, the geometric distribution is nearly uniform across $K$ frames ($\hat\lambda_1 / \hat\lambda_K \approx 1.5$), so PonderNet starts as a near-uniform average and gradually learns to specialize.

\paragraph{Per-sublayer aggregation and broadcast.}
After PonderNet aggregation, the result is a single vector per token (collapsing $K$ frames to 1).
This is projected through $\text{OutProj}: D \to D$ (without bias), then \emph{broadcast back to $K$ frames} for residual addition.
This mechanism allows the SNN sublayer's temporal dynamics (within $K$ frames) to produce a token-level effect that feeds into the residual stream.

\section{Stabilization and Efficient Implementation}
\label{sec:implementation}

\subsection{Residual Centering}
\label{sec:residual-centering}

Each sublayer's output projection is mean-subtracted before residual addition:
\begin{equation}
    \text{center}(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{x} - \frac{1}{D} \sum_{d=1}^{D} x_d
    \label{eq:centering}
\end{equation}
This eliminates DC drift that would otherwise accumulate across 20 residual layers, preventing the residual stream from developing a large constant offset that saturates downstream RMSNorm and PLIFNode inputs.

\subsection{Lateral Inhibition Normalization}
\label{sec:lateral-inhibition}

The output layer uses \textbf{lateral inhibition} (divisive normalization) instead of standard RMSNorm:
\begin{equation}
    \text{LateralInhib}(\mathbf{h}) = \gamma \cdot \frac{\mathbf{h}}{\sqrt{\frac{1}{D}\sum_d h_d^2 + \epsilon}}
    \label{eq:lateral-inhib}
\end{equation}
where $\gamma \in \R^D$ is a learnable gain.
This is mathematically equivalent to RMSNorm~\citep{zhang2019root} but corresponds to a well-established neural computation: inhibitory interneuron pools computing population activity, which divisively normalizes excitatory neuron responses~\citep{carandini2012normalization}.
We implement it as a fused Triton kernel (forward and backward each in a single launch).

\subsection{Triton Fused PLIF Kernels}
\label{sec:triton}

The PLIF recurrence (Eqs.~\ref{eq:plif-charge}--\ref{eq:plif-reset}) involves a sequential scan that cannot be trivially parallelized due to the spike-and-reset nonlinearity.
We implement two variants of fused Triton~\citep{tillet2019triton} kernels:

\paragraph{Per-element kernel.}
For SelectivePLIFNode where $\beta[k], \vth[k]$ vary across $K$ steps.
Each thread block processes \texttt{BLOCK=128} columns across all $K$ sequential steps:
\begin{algorithmic}
\STATE $v \leftarrow v_{\text{init}}$
\FOR{$k = 0, \ldots, K-1$}
    \STATE $v_{\text{pre}} \leftarrow \beta[k] \cdot v + u[k]$ \COMMENT{charge}
    \STATE $s \leftarrow \mathbf{1}[v_{\text{pre}} \geq \vth[k]]$ \COMMENT{fire}
    \STATE $v \leftarrow v_{\text{pre}} - \vth[k] \cdot s$ \COMMENT{soft reset}
\ENDFOR
\end{algorithmic}
The backward kernel performs reverse accumulation with inline Sigmoid surrogate gradient $\sigma'_\alpha(x) = \alpha \cdot \sigma(\alpha x) \cdot (1 - \sigma(\alpha x))$, computing $\nabla_\beta, \nabla_u, \nabla_{\vth}, \nabla_{v_{\text{init}}}$ in a single pass.
All arithmetic uses fp32 accumulation with bf16 storage, preventing precision loss across the $K$-step scan.

\paragraph{Row-parameter kernel.}
For PLIFNode where $\beta, \vth$ are constant across $K$ steps.
These parameters are loaded \emph{once} into registers before the $K$-step loop, reducing global memory reads from 3 per step (beta, u, v\_th) to 1 (u only).
The backward kernel accumulates $\nabla_\beta$ and $\nabla_{\vth}$ in registers across all $K$ steps (single write at the end), further reducing memory traffic.

\paragraph{CPU fallback.}
When Triton is unavailable, we use a 3-phase approach: (1) linear trajectory via Hillis-Steele parallel prefix scan~\citep{blelloch1990prefix, martin2018parallelizing}, (2) spike fixed-point iteration (detached, max 3 iterations), (3) re-computation of $\vpost$ with surrogate gradient.

\subsection{Natural Gradient Compensation}
\label{sec:natural-gradient}

The modulation biases $\mathbf{b}_\beta, \mathbf{b}_\alpha, \mathbf{b}_{\text{th}}$ suffer from two gradient pathologies.
We apply compensation \emph{after} gradient unscaling and \emph{before} gradient clipping:

\paragraph{Phase 1: Activation saturation compensation.}
$\beta = \sigma(b_\beta)$ creates gradient vanishing when $\beta \to 0$ or $1$ ($\sigma'(b_\beta) = \beta(1-\beta) \to 0$).
We compensate by dividing:
\begin{equation}
    \nabla_{b_\beta} \leftarrow \nabla_{b_\beta} \big/ \max\big(\beta(1-\beta),\; 1/C_{\max}\big)
\end{equation}
effectively performing gradient descent in $\beta$-space.
Similarly, $\alpha = \text{softplus}(b_\alpha)$: $\nabla_{b_\alpha} \leftarrow \nabla_{b_\alpha} / \max(\sigma(b_\alpha), 0.1)$.

\paragraph{Phase 2: Cross-layer gradient equalization.}
The residual connection $\mathbf{h} \leftarrow \mathbf{h} + f(\mathbf{h})$ causes reverse-mode gradients to accumulate multiplicatively: shallow layers receive $\sim$20$\times$ larger modulation gradients than deep layers for $L=20$.
For each modulation parameter type ($b_\beta, b_\alpha, b_\theta$), we compute the per-layer gradient norm and normalize to the \textbf{geometric mean}:
\begin{equation}
    \nabla_{\text{layer}_i} \leftarrow \nabla_{\text{layer}_i} \cdot \frac{\text{GeoMean}(\|\nabla_1\|, \ldots, \|\nabla_L\|)}{\|\nabla_i\|}
\end{equation}
The geometric mean (computed in log space) is robust to the exponential spread of norm values.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Setup}

\paragraph{Model configuration.}
\model{}-0.9B has $D=896$, $N=8$, $K=16$, $L=20$, $D_{\text{ff}}=2688$, and a 6144-token BPE~\citep{sennrich2016neural} vocabulary, totaling 874M trainable parameters.

\paragraph{Datasets.}
\emph{Pretraining}: Seq-Monkey~\citep{seqmonkey2023}, a Chinese general corpus with $\sim$29M samples ($\sim$10B tokens at 512-token length).
\emph{SFT}: BelleGroup train\_3.5M\_CN~\citep{bellegroup2023}, containing $\sim$3.5 million Chinese instruction-following conversations in ChatML format.

\paragraph{Compute constraints.}
All training is performed on a single NVIDIA DGX Spark (4$\times$ GB10 GPU, 128GB unified memory).
Due to this limited compute budget, we train on \textbf{small subsets} of both datasets:

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{SNN--SSM duality.}
The selective PLIF dynamics (Eqs.~\ref{eq:selective-charge}--\ref{eq:selective-reset}) establish a direct correspondence with Mamba's selective SSM:
$\beta(t) \leftrightarrow \bar{A}(t)$, $\alpha(t) \leftrightarrow \bar{B}(t)$, $V[t] \leftrightarrow h[t]$.
The key difference is the spike-and-reset mechanism: when $V$ exceeds $\vth(t)$, a spike is emitted and $V$ is reduced by $\vth(t)$.
This introduces a hard, input-dependent nonlinearity at every step, absent in continuous SSMs.
Whether this discrete dynamics provides additional expressiveness or serves as a useful regularizer compared to continuous SSMs is an important open question.

\paragraph{Leakage vs.\ membrane potential.}
Using $(1-\beta) \cdot \vpost$ instead of $\vpost$ as inter-layer activation is algebraically equivalent (downstream weights compensate via $W[:, j] \leftarrow W[:, j] / (1-\beta_j)$).
Empirically, converted and original models produce \emph{token-identical} outputs under greedy decoding.
The leakage formulation distributes signal magnitudes more uniformly across neurons with different time constants, which may improve numerical conditioning of downstream projections.

\paragraph{Data efficiency.}
A notable observation is that \model{} acquires basic language capabilities with only $\sim$14\% of pretraining data and $\sim$1.2\% of SFT data.
This suggests that the SNN selective state space architecture may have favorable inductive biases for language learning, though we caution against strong conclusions given the limited scale and the absence of controlled Transformer baselines at the same data budget.
We plan to train on the full datasets and add quantitative benchmarks in future work.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
(1) At 0.9B parameters and 512-token context, \model{} is significantly smaller than state-of-the-art LLMs.
(2) We lack quantitative benchmark results (e.g., C-Eval, CMMLU) and controlled comparisons with Transformer baselines at matched parameter count and data budget.
(3) The model was trained on Chinese data only and does not support other languages.
(4) Repetition artifacts and limited factual knowledge are present, typical at this scale.
Future work should address scaling, multilingual support, and rigorous benchmarking.

\paragraph{Energy efficiency.}
While \model{} is trained using standard GPU floating-point computation, the spike-based hidden computation could in principle be deployed on neuromorphic chips (e.g., Intel Loihi~\citep{davies2018loihi}) for orders-of-magnitude energy reduction.
Quantifying this potential is left to future work.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have presented \model{}, a 0.9B-parameter language model built entirely on spiking neural networks.
By connecting SNN membrane dynamics to selective state space models and introducing leakage activation, PonderNet adaptive timesteps, fused Triton PLIF kernels, residual centering, lateral inhibition normalization, and natural gradient compensation, we demonstrate that pure SNN architectures can learn language from scratch --- even with limited training data.

\section{Structured Initialization Details}
\label{app:init}

The SNNBlock modulation parameters require careful initialization to ensure stable training from scratch.
We target a reference configuration of $K_{\text{ref}} = 16$ steps and assumed input firing rate $p = 0.15$.

\paragraph{Multi-timescale $\beta$.}
We define $N$ target decay rates $\beta_n = \text{linspace}(0.80, 0.99, N)$, covering fast ($\beta=0.80$, $\tau \approx 5$ steps) to slow ($\beta=0.99$, $\tau \approx 100$ steps) dynamics.
The bias is set to the logit: $b_{\beta,\,n} = \log(\beta_n / (1 - \beta_n))$, repeated across $D$ channels with $\mathcal{N}(0, 0.1)$ perturbation.

\paragraph{$\alpha$ near unity.}
$b_\alpha \sim \mathcal{N}(0.5413, 0.1)$, giving $\alpha = \text{softplus}(b_\alpha) \approx 1.0 \pm 0.15$.

\paragraph{Threshold calibration.}
The stationary membrane voltage variance for a neuron with decay $\beta$ receiving Bernoulli($p$) spike input is:
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_V(\beta) = \sqrt{\frac{p}{3}} \cdot \sqrt{1 - \beta^{2K_{\text{ref}}}}
\end{equation}
We target firing rates $p_{\text{fire},\,n} = \text{linspace}(0.25, 0.08, N)$ (fast neurons fire more).
The threshold is set to achieve this firing rate under a Gaussian approximation:
\begin{equation}
    V_{\text{th},n} = \sigma_V(\beta_n) \cdot \Phi^{-1}(1 - p_{\text{fire},n})
\end{equation}
where $\Phi^{-1}$ is the inverse normal CDF.
The bias $b_{\text{th},n} = \max(V_{\text{th},n} - \vmin, 0.05)$, with per-dimension $\mathcal{N}(0, 0.02)$ perturbation.

\paragraph{$W_{\text{in}}$ time-scale scaling.}
Row $n$ of $W_{\text{in}}$ is scaled by $\sqrt{1 - \beta_n^2}$, normalizing the steady-state input contribution across time scales.

\paragraph{$W_{\text{out}}$ firing-rate balancing.}
Column $n$ of $W_{\text{out}}$ is scaled by $1/\sqrt{p_{\text{fire},\,n}}$ (normalized to mean 1), ensuring equal expected output magnitude regardless of firing rate.

\section{Engineering Optimizations}
\label{app:engineering}

The following optimizations reduce kernel launch overhead and memory traffic but do not change the model's mathematical behavior:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Fused modulation kernel}: The six element-wise activations in SNNBlock ($\sigma, \text{softplus}, |\cdot|, \times$) are fused into a single \texttt{torch.compile(backend='inductor')} kernel, eliminating 7--8 separate CUDA launches.

    \item \textbf{Fused halt weight kernel}: PonderNet's $\sigma \to \log(1-p) \to \text{cumsum} \to \exp \to \text{normalize}$ chain is similarly fused via \texttt{torch.compile}.

    \item \textbf{Merged SNNFFN matmul}: $W_{\text{gate}}$ and $W_{\text{up}}$ are concatenated into a single $(2D_{\text{ff}}, D)$ matrix, reducing two matmul launches to one.

    \item \textbf{Merged PLIF scan}: The gate and up PLIF neurons ($D_{\text{ff}}$ each) are merged into a single $2D_{\text{ff}}$-dim row-parameter scan, halving kernel launch overhead.

    \item \textbf{Gradient checkpointing}: Each of the $L=20$ decoder layers is wrapped in \texttt{torch.utils.checkpoint}, trading recomputation for $\sim$60\% memory reduction.
\end{itemize}
